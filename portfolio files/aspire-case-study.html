Fragmented Information, Lost Opportunities

International students face a critical challenge: they need accurate, timely information about visas, housing, financial aid, campus resources, and cultural integration‚Äîbut this information is scattered across dozens of unreliable sources. The result? Missed deadlines, poor decisions, and unnecessary stress during an already overwhelming transition.


Through extensive user research, I discovered that the core issue wasn't lack of information‚Äîit was the lack of a single, trusted source that could guide students through their entire journey with confidence.

Research & Discovery

01
User Interviews
Conducted 100+ interviews with international students to understand their information-seeking behaviors, pain points with existing resources, and moments of critical decision-making during their academic journey.
02
Gap Analysis
Identified that students spent hours cross-referencing multiple sources but still lacked confidence in the accuracy of information. Discovered high-stakes decisions were being made based on fragmented or outdated guidance.
03
Trust Mapping
Analyzed which sources students trusted most and why. Found that official university resources were trusted but hard to navigate, while peer recommendations were accessible but unreliable.

Reflections & Insights

üéØ

The Information Architecture Challenge

My biggest learning from Aspire was understanding that organizing information is fundamentally a product problem, not just a design problem. I initially organized content by topic (visas, housing, academics)‚Äîbut users didn't think in those categories. They thought in terms of their journey: "I just got accepted, what do I do first?"


Reorganizing around journey stages rather than content types reduced time-to-information by 40%. This taught me that effective product strategy requires deeply understanding not just what users need, but when and why they need it.

ü§ù

Stakeholder Management Reality

Working with multiple university departments taught me that stakeholders often have competing priorities‚Äîand that's okay. The international student office wanted comprehensive information; the admissions office wanted streamlined content to reduce applicant anxiety. Both were right.


I learned to find solutions that satisfied multiple stakeholder needs simultaneously. We created progressive disclosure: simple guidance upfront with "learn more" links for students who wanted depth. This experience taught me that great PMs find creative solutions rather than simply negotiating compromises.

üìä

The Trust Problem is a Product Problem

Students didn't just need information‚Äîthey needed trusted information. I initially underestimated how much source credibility mattered. Through user research, I learned that students would rather spend hours finding "official" sources than use faster but unverified ones.


This insight reshaped our entire product strategy. We added visible verification badges, partnered directly with official departments, and showed last-updated timestamps. These trust signals became core product features, not just nice-to-haves. It reinforced that successful products must address emotional needs (feeling confident and safe) alongside functional ones.

üîÑ

What I'd Do Differently

I would have invested more time in post-launch content maintenance strategy. We built a great discovery experience, but keeping information current required ongoing effort from partner departments. In retrospect, I should have built content governance processes into the product roadmap from the start, not as an afterthought. Future projects will include sustainability planning as a core product requirement, not just a launch consideration.
