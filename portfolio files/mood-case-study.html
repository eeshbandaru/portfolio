The Mental Health Support Gap

Through extensive user research with UC Davis students, I identified a critical gap in mental health support: existing resources were either inaccessible (requiring appointments and long wait times) or inconsistent (relying on students to remember to check in with themselves). Students needed a lightweight, judgment-free tool that met them where they were‚Äîevery single day.


The challenge wasn't just building a mood tracker. It was creating a product that fostered genuine habit formation and supported sustainable mental health practices through thoughtful interaction design and content strategy.

Reflections & Personal Growth

üí≠

Designing for Vulnerability

Building a mental health product taught me that traditional engagement metrics don't tell the whole story. High daily active users might sound great, but what if users feel pressured to check in? I learned to balance growth goals with user wellbeing‚Äîsometimes the most ethical product decision is designing features that people can disengage from without guilt.


This reframed how I think about "stickiness" in consumer products. For Moo'd, success wasn't just retention‚Äîit was building a tool people trusted enough to use during their most vulnerable moments, then felt safe stepping away from when they didn't need it.

The Notification Dilemma

My biggest challenge was optimizing notification timing without becoming intrusive. Early versions prompted check-ins three times daily, which some users found helpful but others found overwhelming. Data showed higher engagement with more prompts‚Äîbut qualitative feedback revealed this was creating anxiety, not supporting mental health.


I learned that quantitative data can mislead when building products for sensitive use cases. We reduced prompts to once daily with user-controlled timing, and although DAU initially dropped 15%, user satisfaction scores increased significantly. This taught me that the "right" metric depends on what you're actually trying to achieve‚Äîand sometimes growth isn't the goal.

üé®

The Power of Non-Judgmental Design

Through user research, I discovered that students avoided journaling apps because they felt judged by their own inconsistency. Seeing blank calendar days or broken streaks made them feel like they were "failing" at self-care. This insight completely changed our approach to progress visualization.


We removed all language around "streaks" and "goals," instead showing patterns and gentle encouragement. This single design decision improved long-term retention by 30%. It reinforced that product language and visual design aren't just aesthetic choices‚Äîthey fundamentally shape user experience and outcomes.

üîÑ

What I'd Approach Differently

I would have consulted with mental health professionals earlier in the product development process. While user research gave us valuable insights, having clinical expertise would have helped us avoid potential pitfalls in our prompting and journaling approaches. Future projects in sensitive domains will include domain experts from day one, not just as reviewers before launch.


I'd also build more robust content moderation for journal entries. While privacy was important, some users wrote concerning content with no pathway to support. Building responsible product features around crisis detection would have been valuable, even though it adds complexity.
