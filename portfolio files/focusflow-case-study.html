Product Strategy

üé® Designing for Sustained Engagement

Rather than maximizing initial downloads, I optimized for repeat usage and long-term habit formation. This meant ruthlessly cutting features that added complexity without supporting the core use case: helping students enter and maintain flow states.

Reflections & Growth

üí° What I Learned About Consumer Product Design

Building FocusFlow taught me that consumer products succeed or fail in the first 30 seconds. My initial design included a detailed onboarding flow explaining features‚Äîbut user testing revealed that students would abandon the app before completing it. They needed to experience value immediately, not learn about it.


The pivot: We stripped onboarding to a single screen and let users start a focus session with one tap. This counterintuitive approach‚Äîshowing less, enabling more‚Äîincreased session completion by 40%. It reinforced a core PM lesson: in consumer products, reducing friction is often more valuable than adding features.


Biggest challenge: Balancing feature requests from power users (who wanted deep customization) with the need to keep the core experience simple for new users. I learned to use data to make these tradeoffs‚Äîlooking at which features drove repeat usage versus which just increased complexity. This taught me that saying "no" to good ideas is sometimes the most important product decision.


What I'd approach differently: I would have defined clearer success metrics from day one. We added engagement KPIs partway through development, which meant some early design decisions weren't optimized for the outcomes we ultimately cared about. Starting with "what does success look like?" would have created better alignment across design, engineering, and product decisions.
