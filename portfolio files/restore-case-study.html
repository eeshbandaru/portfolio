The Market Opportunity

A $12B Market Blocked by Trust Paralysis

The second-life EV battery market represents a massive sustainability opportunity, but consumer adoption remains below 5%. The problem isn't demand‚Äîit's trust. Buyers can't evaluate the safety and longevity of repurposed batteries, leading to decision paralysis and abandoned purchases.


Through 50+ user interviews, we discovered that 68% of potential buyers abandon purchases not because they lack information, but because they can't translate technical specs (2,847 cycles, 87% capacity) into what matters: "Is this safe for my garage?" and "Will this last 5 years?"

Product Hypothesis

üí°
Translating Complexity Into Trust

If we translate complex battery health data into clear trust signals at the point of decision, we can unlock mainstream adoption by removing cognitive burden from high-stakes purchases.


The solution: A real-time tiered trust system that converts technical metrics into three simple signals: Safety (UL certification badge), Lifespan (5-8 years vs. cycle counts), and Seller credibility (verification score). Progressive disclosure allows power users to dive deeper while keeping the core experience simple.

Strategic Choices

üéØ
What We Chose NOT to Build

Peer comparison tools: Testing showed comparing 8+ options increased decision paralysis. We prioritized single-listing trust for v1 over multi-listing comparison features.


Community reviews: 80% of sellers were first-time with no review history. We invested in third-party certification instead of building a reviews system that would be empty at launch.


Price optimization: Research showed trust mattered 3x more than price in purchase decisions. We focused resources on trust signals rather than pricing tools, delivering better ROI.

Reflections & Personal Growth

The Power of Rapid Testing

My biggest learning was the value of killing ideas fast. We spent 3 weeks building v1, then killed it after 15 user tests. This felt painful at the time, but it saved us months of building the wrong thing. Every subsequent pivot came from user feedback, not intuition.

Key insight: The teams that ship fastest aren't necessarily the ones that code fastest‚Äîthey're the ones that validate fastest. Our AI-powered workflow (Claude for research synthesis, v0 for prototyping) let us test 5 versions where traditional teams would commit to 1. This velocity multiplier was the difference between finding product-market fit and guessing.

ü§ù

Navigating Stakeholder Complexity

Working with external partners (UL for certification) taught me that stakeholder alignment isn't about consensus‚Äîit's about clarity. UL cared about maintaining certification standards; we cared about speed to market. The breakthrough came when we stopped trying to compromise and started mapping out what each party absolutely needed vs. what was negotiable.

We automated 95% of the verification process while maintaining UL's quality standards by focusing on edge case handling rather than trying to automate everything perfectly. This experience taught me that successful B2B2C products require thinking in layers: what does each stakeholder actually need to say yes?

üìä

When Data Should Override Intuition

I initially believed buyers wanted exhaustive comparison tools‚Äîit seemed obvious that more information = better decisions. User testing proved me completely wrong. When we showed 8+ battery options, decision paralysis increased. The data was clear: trust-first, single-listing optimization beat feature-rich comparison.

This taught me to hold my product instincts loosely and data tightly. AI accelerated this learning loop: we could synthesize 50 interviews in hours, spot patterns I'd have missed, and test hypotheses same-day. The best product decisions came from when I used AI to challenge my assumptions, not just validate them.

üîÑ

What I'd Do Differently

Test pricing earlier: We validated trust extensively but didn't prototype pricing tiers until late in the process. I should have tested trust-price trade-offs simultaneously‚Äîwould users pay more for higher trust scores? This gap meant we shipped without clear monetization validation.

Segment research from day one: Early interviews lumped all buyers together. Only later did we realize first-time buyers, repeat purchasers, and DIY enthusiasts had completely different needs. If I could redo it, I'd segment users in the first 10 interviews, not after 30.

Build seller tools sooner: We obsessed over buyer UX but neglected seller experience. A simple dashboard showing "add UL cert to go from 6.8 ‚Üí 8.4 trust score" would have improved inventory quality faster. Future projects will treat both sides of the marketplace as equally important from day one.
